AI-First Banking Architecture & Use Cases

1 Introduction

The mainframe revolutionized transaction processing. The cloud enabled elastic scaling. Now AI is fundamentally changing what banks can automate.

The difference between Generative AI and Agentic AI is the difference between a tool that drafts an email and one that reads your inbox, prioritizes responses, and handles routine inquiries without supervision. Generative AI creates content. Agentic AI executes work.

Banks that are building agent-driven architectures will respond faster, serve customers better, and allocate capital more efficiently than competitors treating AI as an add-on.

To understand where banking AI is headed, we need to see how banking architecture evolved to support increasingly intelligent automation—and what makes the current moment different from past technology transitions.

2 Technology Transitions in Banking Architecture

1960s–2000s: From Mainframes to Services

Banking technology evolved from centralized mainframe systems—where deposits, payments, and accounting lived in a single computing environment—to distributed architectures that separated customer channels from core processing. The internet brought online banking and digital access. Service-oriented architecture (SOA) broke monolithic systems into reusable components like KYC, payments, and fraud checks. By the early 2000s, banks had built the foundational separation between presentation, application logic, and data that modern systems still rely on.

2010s: Cloud and API Ecosystems

Cloud platforms introduced elastic scaling and microservices, allowing banks to build and deploy products faster. This era enabled open banking, partner integrations, and modern data platforms. More importantly, it established API-based connectivity as the standard way systems communicate—a prerequisite for agent-driven architectures.

2022–Present: AI as Operating Infrastructure

The emergence of large language models changed what banks can automate. Earlier AI handled narrow tasks like fraud scoring or chatbot responses. Today’s systems combine language models with enterprise data to support complex decision-making and multi-step workflows.

Three architectural patterns define this shift:

  • Structured context: Banks feed private data to AI models through governed pipelines rather than training custom models, allowing agents to work with proprietary information while maintaining control

  • Tool integration: AI systems can now trigger actions across multiple platforms—reading documents, querying databases, initiating workflows—rather than just generating text responses

  • Semantic retrieval: Vector databases allow agents to find relevant information based on meaning rather than keywords, making knowledge discovery faster and more accurate

This combination—language understanding plus structured data access plus action capabilities—is what enables AI agents to execute work rather than simply provide information.

3 The Three Levels of Agents in Banking

AI agents differ by autonomy level. L1 agents provide intelligence, L2 agents execute processes, and L3 agents make decisions independently. Understanding these levels helps banks deploy agents appropriately—matching capability to risk tolerance and regulatory requirements.

L1 – Insight Agents (Assistive Intelligence)

L1 agents extract and synthesize information to support human decision-making. They assist with planning, discovery, and knowledge retrieval but do not take actions independently. Humans control all execution.

Morgan Stanley’s AI @ Morgan Stanley Debrief tool automates meeting notes, analyzes client profiles and transaction histories, and generates tailored recommendations before advisor meetings. The tool saves approximately 30 minutes per meeting, freeing 10–15 hours per week for higher-value client work.

Morgan Stanley’s AskResearchGPT synthesizes insights from over 70,000 proprietary research reports. Similar tools have reduced document review time by up to 90% while improving accuracy by 25%.

L2 – Process Orchestration Agents (Semi-Autonomous)

L2 agents execute multi-step, rule-governed business processes. They decompose tasks, orchestrate workflows across systems, and automate structured operations while operating under human supervision and regulatory controls.

Compliance documentation illustrates this level. Writing suspicious activity reports (SARs), regulatory memos, and audit summaries traditionally requires manual effort and creates consistency problems. L2 agents—combining generative AI with intelligent document processing—automate classification, extraction, summarization, and drafting while enforcing compliance standards. These systems have cut turnaround time by 80% and improved processing accuracy, allowing compliance teams to handle higher volumes with better quality.

Unlike L1 agents that provide recommendations, L2 agents generate final work products and execute regulated workflows within defined guardrails.

L3 – Complex Decisioning Agents (Fully Autonomous)

L3 agents continuously monitor their environment, make decisions, and trigger actions without constant human involvement. They coordinate L1 and L2 agents to operate as closed-loop decision systems.

Autonomous mortgage optimization shows L3 in practice. Rather than waiting for borrowers or advisors to identify refinancing opportunities, an L3 agent monitors market rates, borrower credit profiles, loan performance, and property valuations continuously. When conditions align, the agent triggers eligibility checks through L2 agents, generates personalized offers, initiates pre-approval workflows, and notifies customers and relationship managers. Work that traditionally requires weeks of coordination compresses into hours.

Because L3 agents act independently, they introduce operational and regulatory risk. Governance, auditability, and human override capabilities are not optional—they’re fundamental requirements.

Implementation Reality: Most banks operate primarily at L1 today and are scaling L2 capabilities. L1 agents prepare intelligence, L2 agents execute regulated processes, and L3 agents make and act on decisions independently. Governed L3 use cases represent the next competitive frontier and the next major governance challenge.

4 Experience Agents vs. Domain Agents

Experience Agents are human-facing agents that interact directly with customers, relationship managers, and operations staff. They are context-aware, maintain conversational or session-level memory, and adapt responses based on user intent, historical interactions, and real-time context. These agents enhance user experience through personalization, guidance, and recommendation, while operating under strict governance and feedback controls appropriate for regulated environments.

Domain Agents are system-facing agents that interact directly with enterprise data, applications, and workflows. These agents range from semi-autonomous to fully autonomous and execute tasks such as validation, orchestration, optimization, and decisioning. They operate within defined business policies, risk controls, and model constraints, and are responsible for executing core banking processes across credit, fraud, compliance, and operations.

5 The Reference Architecture for an AI-First Bank

The reference architecture for an intelligent, AI-first bank is organized into distinct but tightly integrated layers. Each layer plays a specific role in enabling Experience Agents, Domain Agents, and autonomous decisioning while maintaining regulatory control and operational resilience.

5.1 Customer Layer

The Customer Layer consists of omnichannel sales and service interfaces across web, mobile, contact centers, and advisor desktops. This is where Experience Agents operate. These agents interact directly with customers and bankers, providing personalization, guidance, recommendations, and conversational support. This layer is optimized for responsiveness, user experience, and secure identity-aware interactions.

5.2 Action Layer

The Action Layer is where business execution occurs and is primarily powered by Domain Agents. Core banking processes—such as trading, cash management, loan origination, servicing, fraud operations, and payments—are decomposed into orchestrated agent-driven workflows. At this layer, business processes are no longer static workflows but dynamic, policy-constrained sequences of agent actions operating across multiple systems.

5.3 Intelligence Layer (AI Fabric)

The Intelligence Layer serves as the orchestration and decisioning fabric for the entire architecture. This is the “control center” for enterprise AI and includes model orchestration, inference runtime, prompt management, hallucination detection, model risk controls, and performance monitoring. It is also where L1, L2, and L3 agents are coordinated, ensuring that insights, workflows, and autonomous decisions operate within defined risk and governance boundaries.

5.4 Data Layer

The Data Layer provides the informational backbone for all agents. It includes traditional enterprise data platforms such as data lakes and warehouses, alongside vector stores for semantic retrieval and feature stores for model-driven decisioning. This layer ensures that agents operate on trusted, governed, and explainable data while enabling both real-time and batch intelligence.

5.5 Foundational Layer

The Foundational Layer consists of the bank’s core systems of record, including deposits, lending, payments, general ledger, and customer master data. These systems remain the ultimate source of truth and continue to anchor financial integrity, regulatory reporting, and transaction processing.

5.6 Control Plane and Cloud Native Fabric

Cutting across all layers is the Control Plane, which provides enterprise-wide security, governance, compliance, auditability, and access control. Alongside it is the Cloud Native Fabric, which enables workloads to be deployed on-premise, in the public cloud, or in hybrid environments. Together, these cross-cutting layers ensure that innovation in AI and agents does not come at the expense of regulatory discipline, data protection, or operational resilience.

6 Use Case 1: Retail Banking (Agent Collaboration)

Scenario: A customer, Grace, interacts with her bank, while a relationship manager, Davis, manages the relationship. This workflow involves multiple Experience Agents and one Domain Agent working in harmony.

The Customer Experience: Grace uses a Spend Analyzer Agent that tracks spending patterns without being restrictive. When she plans for a major expense, a Product Recommendation Agent identifies that she is likely to face a future cash shortfall and suggests relevant lending products. A Personalized Repayment Plan Agent then creates a repayment schedule based on her specific income and expense patterns.

The Banker Experience: Davis works through an AI-powered dashboard. An Application Tracker Agent summarizes Grace’s loan application and categorizes it by risk tier and approval probability. A Credit Decisioning Agent assists with due diligence and issues an automated approval within predefined risk and policy thresholds, routing exceptions to human credit officers for manual review.

This use case demonstrates how AI integrates seamlessly into the customer’s financial life while empowering bankers with summarized, actionable, and policy-compliant intelligence.

7 Use Case 2: Corporate Banking (Agent-to-Agent Automation)

Scenario: Supremo Corporation plans to expand into Europe, requiring complex coordination between the corporate client and the bank across multiple geographies.

Forecasting: The CFO uses a Cash Flow Forecasting Agent that consolidates data from Balance Inquiry Agents and Document Extraction Agents that read and interpret invoice data. Together, these agents project a future working capital shortfall.

Agent-to-Agent Communication: The client’s agent communicates directly with the bank’s domain agents to request a €50 million credit facility.

Execution: The bank’s Risk Agent generates a detailed credit write-up by analyzing financial statements, market conditions, and unstructured reports. Once approved, the Supply Chain Finance (SCF) Smart Program Agent reads supplier contracts and automatically onboards new European suppliers into the financing program.

All approvals and onboarding activities operate within predefined regulatory, legal, and credit policy constraints, with automatic escalation to regional credit officers when cross-border risk thresholds are exceeded.

What traditionally takes months of email exchanges, document reviews, and manual coordination is executed rapidly through interoperable, governed domain agents.

8 Use Case 3: Risk Management (Stress Testing and CCAR)

Scenario: Agentic AI is applied to core “Run the Bank” risk processes, specifically regulatory stress testing and scenario analysis.

The Problem: Preparing for regulatory stress tests traditionally takes months of data gathering, model execution, and scenario construction to answer questions such as, “What happens if interest rates rise by 2 percent?”

The Solution: An AI Assistant retrieves Federal Reserve guidance and maps regulatory stress scenarios into calibrated internal baseline and adverse frameworks. A Stress Testing Domain Agent then applies these scenarios directly to the bank’s portfolio using enterprise calculation engines.

Result: The Chief Risk Officer can review capital adequacy, liquidity impact, and profitability projections in a single day rather than after multiple months of manual preparation.

9 Use Case 4: Financial Crime (AI Investigator)

Scenario: Managing anti-money laundering alerts where high false-positive rates traditionally require large investigation teams.

The Process: The AI Investigator Agent reviews transaction alerts in real time. It assigns a quantitative risk score (for example, 98.85) and generates a transparent narrative explaining the suspicious behavior, such as rapid movement of funds or transaction structuring.

Human-in-the-Loop: Investigators no longer spend hours manually gathering evidence. Instead, they review the AI-generated narrative along with a visual “waterfall” depiction of fund flows. They validate the insight and mark the case as credible or non-credible, feeding this feedback back into continuous model improvement.

Outcome: A task that previously took days of manual evidence gathering is condensed into minutes, producing regulator-ready, auditable, and evidence-backed cases.

10 Conclusion

The shift to Agentic AI is the defining characteristic of Banking 4.0. It enables banks to move beyond simply recording transactions toward actively orchestrating financial outcomes in real time. Yet trust remains the true currency of banking. As agents take on greater responsibility, the Intelligence Layer—which governs orchestration, hallucination controls, model risk management, and auditability—becomes the most critical piece of infrastructure in the modern bank. The institutions that win will be those that successfully combine the creative power of large language models (LLMs) in the front office with the speed, privacy, and precision of small language models (SLMs) in the back office—delivering innovation without compromising control.
